The Workshop

The public front door: learn what AI can do, use it safely, bring stuck problems, and build practical tools with human review.

Learn

AI literacy for people first

Not just executives, founders, or civic insiders. The first audience is residents who are most likely to be acted on by AI without being invited into the room.

  • Seniors, parents, students, renters, workers, disabled neighbors, and low-literacy residents.
  • What AI is good at, bad at, and too risky for.
  • Privacy, scams, bias, hallucinations, and when to ask a human.
Solve

Problem-solving clinics

Bring a real problem. Leave with a clearer brief, useful context, a stakeholder map, and the next practical move.

  • Service navigation and civic issue triage.
  • Neighborhood association operations.
  • Documents, forms, phone trees, and process bottlenecks.
Build

Tool-building workshops

Learning should advance. People should be able to move from "what is this?" to "I helped build something useful." Tiny miracle, fewer slides.

  • Guided build nights for forms, trackers, local guides, and simple automations.
  • Apprentice-to-builder levels, with real portfolio artifacts.
  • Community-benefit tools first; commercial language later, if earned.

A path of advancement

The Workshop should not be a one-off “Intro to AI” class. It should give people a ladder.

Level 1 · Safe user: ask better questions, protect private data, spot scams, verify outputs.
Level 2 · Local problem framer: turn a messy issue into a brief, checklist, resource map, or 311 follow-up plan.
Level 3 · Tool builder: build a small assistant, tracker, intake form, or neighborhood guide with supervision.
Level 4 · Workshop helper: help others learn, document patterns, and maintain shared resources.

The Loom

The shared civic memory layer. Start with data we can fetch, verify, and explain.

Build first

Public-data spine

The Loom should begin with the practical civic data already described in the build guide, then become useful through Workshop questions.

  • Kansas City 311 requests and follow-up state.
  • City permits, violations, dangerous buildings, crime reports, budgets, vendor payments, zoning, council agendas, and votes.
  • County and state records where they affect Waldo: property, courts, elections, agencies, representatives, and public programs.
Local context

Waldo resources and assets

The Loom is not only government data. It should know the living neighborhood.

  • Neighborhood associations, libraries, churches, schools, clinics, nonprofits, mutual aid groups, makerspaces, and trusted local businesses.
  • Meeting notes, commitments, recurring problems, and who knows what.
  • Resource maps for residents who need help now, not after a procurement cycle wakes up.

The existing NeighborhoodOS build guide already covers the KC Open Data pipeline: fetch 311, permits, crime, violations, dangerous buildings, budget, vendor payments, ingest to SQLite, generate digests, and ask plain-English questions with local models.

The Grove

Community-governed access to AI capacity, spaces, tools, and rules.

The Grove is the local stewardship layer. It can include shared devices, trusted access points, workshops, compute, partner spaces, and operating agreements. The point is not to brag about servers. The point is that people have a governed place to learn and use AI without being harvested, priced out, or talked down to.

90-day Waldo pilot

Small enough to run. Useful enough to learn from.

  • 3 Learn sessions for residents first, with special attention to vulnerable people and people most exposed to automated systems.
  • 2 Solve clinics for real problems from residents, neighborhood groups, and local organizations.
  • 2 advanced Build workshops where participants make simple, useful tools from actual local needs.
  • 1 Loom seed built from 311, city, county, state, and Waldo resource data.
  • 1 candidate tool only if the clinics reveal a clear need. A service navigator and action tracker is the current best example.

WaldoNet was the prototype name. Waldo Grove is the current pilot frame. NeighborhoodOS is the broader model.
