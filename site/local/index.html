Neighborhood signal board

The old page was a civic intelligence dashboard. This version keeps that shape, but makes the purpose clearer: every signal either explains what is happening nearby or helps choose the next wedge.

311 service pressureUse KCMO open 311 data to spot unresolved property, trash, street, water, animal-control, and nuisance issues around the node.
Development and zoning changesWatch City Plan Commission dockets, staff reports, rezonings, permits, TIF, and public hearings before neighbors hear about them too late.
Budget and capital plansTrack city budget lines, PIAC/GO KC style capital work, and county budget items that affect streets, parks, public safety, and property taxes.
City council and committee actionPull Legistar/city clerk agendas into plain English: what matters, where it is, who sponsors it, and what neighbors can still do.
County legislature and property tax signalsJackson County meetings, budgets, assessments, and tax deadlines matter locally. The node should put them next to city signals, not in a separate maze.

The wedge: home maintenance brigade

The local node is not just a dashboard. Dashboards become civic aquarium gravel if they do not lead to action. The first Waldo wedge is practical: once a month, neighbors pick one owner-occupied home that needs one doable repair and fix it together.

Why this wedge fits the dashboard311, code complaints, dangerous-building flags, aging housing, and property-tax pressure all point at the same local pain: homes quietly falling apart.
What software handlesIntake, eligibility, volunteer skills, supply lists, donations, schedule, photo log, follow-up tasks, and public memory.
What neighbors handleChoosing the house, doing the work, feeding the crew, deciding what counts as safe, fair, and worth repeating.

Governance promise

The node should be boringly accountable.

Rules for v1: cite sources, show dates, keep raw links, never pretend stale data is fresh, and make it clear when an AI summary is a summary rather than an official record.
